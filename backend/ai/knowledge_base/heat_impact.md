# Heat Impact: How Solar Radiation Heats Your Home's Facades

## Solar Radiation and Building Surfaces

When sunlight strikes a building's exterior surface, energy is either reflected, absorbed, or transmitted. Dark-colored walls and roofs absorb up to 90% of incident solar radiation and convert it to heat. Light-colored or reflective surfaces may reflect 70-80% of that energy. The absorbed heat conducts inward through the wall assembly, raising interior temperatures over time.

This process — called **solar heat gain** — is responsible for a significant portion of summer cooling loads in residential buildings. In warm climates, solar heat gain through walls and windows can account for 25-50% of total cooling energy use.

## How Different Facade Orientations Behave

The four cardinal directions of a home receive solar radiation very differently:

### South-Facing Walls and Windows
In the Northern Hemisphere, south-facing surfaces receive the most annual solar energy. In summer, the high sun angle means most direct radiation hits the roof rather than the south wall — making south walls relatively manageable with roof overhangs. In winter, the low sun angle drives strong solar gain through south windows, which is a benefit in cold climates (passive solar heating).

### West-Facing Walls and Windows
West-facing surfaces are the most problematic in summer. The afternoon sun is still intense but lower in the sky, striking west walls directly at a time when outdoor temperatures are already at their daily peak. Interior temperatures on west-facing rooms often run 5-10degF warmer than north-facing rooms on summer afternoons. Homeowners often notice west-facing rooms are the hardest to cool.

### East-Facing Walls
East walls receive morning sun when temperatures are cooler. While solar gain is still present, the combination of lower ambient temperature and shorter daily exposure makes east walls less of a cooling problem than west walls.

### North-Facing Walls
North-facing walls receive almost no direct sunlight in temperate climates. They stay cooler but are more prone to moisture issues (see mold and humidity document). They provide the most thermally stable interior environment in summer.

## Thermal Mass and Delayed Heat Transfer

**Thermal mass** refers to a material's ability to absorb and store heat before releasing it. High-mass materials like concrete, brick, and stone absorb heat slowly during the day and release it slowly at night. Low-mass materials like metal siding heat up and cool down rapidly.

High thermal mass on a west-facing wall can delay peak interior heat gain by 4-8 hours — turning a 3 PM wall temperature spike into a 9-11 PM interior heat release. In climates with cool nights, this delay allows the building to vent stored heat before the next day's solar cycle begins.

## Window Orientation and Solar Gain Coefficients

Windows are the weak point in the building envelope for solar heat gain. A standard double-pane window has a Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC) between 0.25 and 0.60. An SHGC of 0.40 means 40% of incident solar radiation passes through the glass as heat.

- South windows with low-e coatings and high SHGC (0.40-0.60) are ideal in cold climates — they admit winter solar heat while limiting summer gain when paired with a proper overhang.
- West and east windows benefit from low SHGC glass (0.20-0.30) to reduce unwanted summer heat gain.
- North windows gain almost nothing from SHGC management; focus on U-factor (insulation value) instead.

## Shading Strategies That Work

Exterior shading is far more effective than interior window treatments because it stops solar radiation before it enters the building.

- **Fixed overhangs**: A properly sized south-facing overhang blocks summer sun (high angle) while admitting winter sun (low angle). The ideal overhang depth is approximately 50-60% of window height for mid-latitude U.S. locations.
- **Exterior blinds and shutters**: Can block 60-90% of solar gain on any orientation.
- **Deciduous trees**: Provide summer shade while allowing winter sun penetration after leaves drop. A single mature shade tree on the west side of a home can reduce summer cooling costs by 10-25%.
- **Roof color**: A cool roof with high solar reflectance can reduce roof surface temperature by 50-70degF compared to a standard dark roof, cutting attic temperatures and cooling loads meaningfully.

## Practical Implications for Homeowners

- If your west-facing rooms are uncomfortably hot in summer, adding exterior shading or low-SHGC window film is more cost-effective than increasing air conditioning capacity.
- Painting a dark-colored facade a lighter color can reduce absorbed solar energy by 30-50%.
- Attic insulation directly above living spaces is one of the highest-return energy investments because it limits downward heat transfer from a sun-baked roof.
