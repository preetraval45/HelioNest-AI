# Solar Basics: Understanding Sun Path, Irradiance, and Peak Solar Hours

## How the Sun Moves Across the Sky

The sun does not follow the same path every day. Its trajectory changes continuously based on the time of year and your geographic location. Two key angles describe the sun's position at any moment:

- **Azimuth**: The compass direction of the sun, measured in degrees from true north (0deg). East is 90deg, south is 180deg, west is 270deg.
- **Elevation (Altitude)**: How high the sun sits above the horizon, measured in degrees. At sunrise and sunset the elevation is 0deg; at solar noon it reaches its daily peak.

Understanding these angles tells you exactly when and where sunlight will strike your home, yard, or roof at any given time of day or year.

## Seasonal Variation in Sun Path

The sun's path shifts dramatically between summer and winter due to Earth's axial tilt of 23.5 degrees.

- **Summer solstice (around June 21)**: The sun rises far to the northeast, climbs to its highest elevation at noon, and sets far to the northwest. Days are longest, and the sun spends more time overhead, delivering maximum energy.
- **Winter solstice (around December 21)**: The sun rises in the southeast, stays low in the sky all day, and sets in the southwest. Days are shortest, and the shallow angle means sunlight travels through more atmosphere before reaching your property.
- **Spring and fall equinoxes**: The sun rises due east and sets due west, with a moderate arc across the sky.

## Latitude Effects

Your latitude — how far north or south you are — is the dominant factor in how high the sun climbs.

- At the equator (0deg latitude), the sun can pass nearly directly overhead.
- In Charlotte, NC (roughly 35deg N), the solar noon elevation peaks around 78deg in summer and dips to about 32deg in winter.
- In northern states like Minnesota (45deg N), the winter sun barely reaches 22deg above the horizon, reducing solar energy availability significantly.

As a rule of thumb, solar noon elevation at summer solstice is approximately **90 minus your latitude plus 23.5 degrees**, and at winter solstice it is **90 minus your latitude minus 23.5 degrees**.

## What Are Peak Solar Hours?

Peak solar hours (PSH) refer to the equivalent number of hours per day when solar irradiance averages 1,000 watts per square meter (W/m2) — the standard test condition for solar panels. A location with 5 PSH receives the same total energy as if it had bright noon sun for exactly 5 hours.

- The U.S. Southwest (Arizona, Nevada) typically sees 6-7 PSH.
- The Southeast (Georgia, Carolinas) averages 4.5-5.5 PSH.
- The Pacific Northwest and New England average 3.5-4.5 PSH.

PSH is the single most important number for sizing a solar panel system.

## What Reduces Solar Irradiance?

Even on sunny days, several factors reduce the solar energy reaching your roof:

- **Cloud cover**: Overcast skies can reduce irradiance to 10-25% of clear-sky values.
- **Atmospheric path length**: Low sun angles in winter force sunlight through far more atmosphere, scattering and absorbing energy.
- **Air pollution and aerosols**: Dust, smoke, and pollution scatter light before it reaches the surface.
- **Panel tilt and orientation**: A south-facing roof tilted at your latitude angle captures the most annual energy. Flat roofs or north-facing surfaces lose 20-40% efficiency.

## Practical Takeaways for Homeowners

- South-facing roofs in the Northern Hemisphere receive the most solar energy year-round and are ideal for solar panels.
- Trees or structures shading your roof even partially in the morning or afternoon can cut solar production by 20-50%.
- Understanding your local PSH helps you estimate how many panels you need to offset your electricity bill.
- Winter months deliver 30-60% less solar energy than summer months, so battery storage or grid backup matters if you want year-round solar independence.
