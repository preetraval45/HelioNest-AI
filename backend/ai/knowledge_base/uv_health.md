# UV Health: UV Index, Skin Exposure Risks, and Protective Measures

## What Is Ultraviolet Radiation?

Ultraviolet (UV) radiation is electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths shorter than visible light (100-400 nanometers). The sun emits three UV bands:

- **UVC (100-280 nm)**: Almost entirely absorbed by the ozone layer and atmosphere. Does not reach Earth's surface in meaningful quantities under normal conditions.
- **UVB (280-315 nm)**: The primary cause of sunburn and a significant driver of skin cancer. Intensity varies strongly with time of day, season, and ozone levels.
- **UVA (315-400 nm)**: Penetrates more deeply into the skin than UVB, causing tanning, premature aging, and contributing to melanoma risk. UVA intensity is far more constant throughout the day and year than UVB.

Approximately 95% of UV radiation reaching Earth's surface is UVA; 5% is UVB. Despite its smaller proportion, UVB is responsible for most acute skin damage.

## The UV Index Scale

The **UV Index (UVI)** is an internationally standardized scale developed by the World Health Organization (WHO) and the United Nations Environment Programme. It quantifies the risk of skin and eye damage from solar UV radiation at the Earth's surface.

| UV Index | Exposure Category | Time to Burn (Fair Skin) | Recommended Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | Low | 60+ minutes | Minimal protection needed |
| 3-5 | Moderate | 30-45 minutes | Apply SPF 30+, wear hat |
| 6-7 | High | 20-25 minutes | SPF 30-50+, seek shade midday |
| 8-10 | Very High | 10-15 minutes | SPF 50+, protective clothing required |
| 11+ | Extreme | Less than 10 minutes | Avoid outdoor exposure 10am-4pm |

The UV Index is reported at solar noon — the daily peak — and assumes clear sky conditions. Clouds reduce UVI (by 25-75% depending on cloud thickness and type), but clouds do NOT eliminate UV risk — UV scatters through overcast skies and reflected off water and sand.

## Factors That Increase UV Exposure

Several factors raise UV intensity beyond the baseline UV index reading:

- **Altitude**: UV increases approximately 4% per 1,000 feet (12% per 1,000 meters) of elevation. A skier at 10,000 feet faces roughly 40% more UV than at sea level.
- **Latitude**: UV intensity is highest near the equator and decreases toward the poles. The continental U.S. ranges from high UV in Florida and the Southwest to moderate in the northern states.
- **Reflection**: Snow reflects 80-90% of UV. Dry sand reflects 15-20%. Water reflects 10-30% depending on angle. Reflected UV causes burns on areas not directly facing the sky — under chins, inside nostrils, lower lips.
- **Ozone depletion**: The stratospheric ozone layer filters UVB. Ozone thinning over polar regions during spring can temporarily raise UVB at high latitudes significantly above normal.
- **Time of day**: 50-75% of daily UV dose occurs between 10 AM and 4 PM. The UV index at 8 AM or 6 PM is typically less than 10% of the noon peak.

## Cumulative UV Dose and Long-Term Health Effects

Skin damage from UV is cumulative over a lifetime. The key health concerns are:

- **Sunburn**: Acute UVB damage causing inflammation, cell death, and DNA damage. A single blistering sunburn in childhood doubles lifetime melanoma risk.
- **Photoaging**: Chronic UVA exposure causes wrinkles, skin laxity, and irregular pigmentation — distinct from chronological aging. Studies attribute approximately 80% of visible facial aging to UV exposure, not biological aging.
- **Skin cancer**: The three most common cancers in the United States are all skin cancers. Basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma are highly curable but locally destructive. Melanoma is the most dangerous form and is strongly linked to both cumulative UV exposure and intense intermittent exposure (sunburns).
- **Cataracts**: Long-term UV exposure to the eyes is the leading modifiable risk factor for cataract formation.

The U.S. sees approximately 5 million skin cancer treatments per year. The Skin Cancer Foundation estimates that 1 in 5 Americans will develop skin cancer by age 70.

## Protective Measures

### Sunscreen
- SPF (Sun Protection Factor) measures UVB protection only. SPF 30 blocks ~97% of UVB; SPF 50 blocks ~98%.
- **Broad-spectrum** labeling indicates UVA protection is also included (required for broad-spectrum designation under FDA rules).
- Apply 1 ounce (a shot glass full) to cover the entire body. Most people apply only 25-50% of the recommended amount, reducing effective SPF by half.
- Reapply every 2 hours and immediately after swimming or heavy sweating.

### Protective Clothing
- Standard summer cotton (white T-shirt, wet) has an SPF equivalent of only 3-7.
- UPF (Ultraviolet Protection Factor) clothing is rated to block specific UV percentages. UPF 50 blocks 98% of UV.
- Wide-brimmed hats (3+ inch brim) reduce facial UV exposure by 50-70%.
- UV-blocking sunglasses (labeled UV400 or 100% UV protection) are essential — unprotected eye exposure raises cataract and macular degeneration risk.

## UV Glass Coatings for Windows

Standard float glass (ordinary window glass) blocks nearly all UVB but transmits 75-80% of UVA. This is why sitting near a sunny window does not cause sunburn but can cause tanning and accelerate photoaging over years of daily exposure.

- **Low-e glass**: Designed primarily for thermal performance, most low-e coatings also reduce UV transmission significantly — UV transmission of 10-30% depending on the specific coating.
- **Laminated glass**: The interlayer (PVB or SGP) in laminated glass blocks virtually all UVA and UVB. Common in automotive windshields (which is why drivers rarely burn through their windshield).
- **Specialty UV-blocking window film**: Aftermarket films can reduce UV transmission through existing windows to less than 1-5% while remaining nearly transparent visually. These protect furnishings, artwork, flooring, and occupants from UV fading and skin damage.

## UV-Sensitive Materials in the Home

Prolonged UV exposure damages many household materials:

- **Wood flooring and furniture**: UV causes bleaching and graying of unfinished wood; darkening and color shift in stained or finished wood. UV-blocking window film is the most effective preventive measure.
- **Fabrics and upholstery**: Dyes fade significantly faster near sunny windows. UV fading of fabric is an irreversible photochemical reaction.
- **Art and photographs**: Museum-quality preservation requires UV-filtering glass in frames and UV-blocking window treatments.
- **Plastics and rubber**: UV causes brittleness, cracking, and chalking. Outdoor furniture, hose materials, and rubber gaskets in south-facing exposures typically have shorter service lives than shaded equivalents.

Addressing UV exposure through window films or low-e glass replacement protects both occupants and home contents simultaneously.
